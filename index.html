What are Pueblos Mágicos?

The Pueblo Mágico program was created to popularize cities and towns which have ‘magical’ attributes. Towns are selected if they can show that they possess culturally significant events, stories, and legends. The program aims to show that the culture in Mexico goes much deeper than what appears on the surface, in the form of local mythology, local history, and family traditions. These values which may not be found in other places in Mexico are what makes them so special (Clausen and Gyimóthy 321).

The Beginning

The first Pueblos Mágicos were established in 2001. The Ministry of Tourism elected 30 towns to participate in the program as a pilot program. This bolstered their historical attributes and allowed an amplification of their culture. The result being an improvement in how they are viewed by surrounding populations as well as visitors (Ramírez and Espinosa 222).

Eligibility

In order to be considered for the program, a town or city must pass through a series of bureaucratic approval processes instantiated by the Mexican Ministry of Tourism. The program includes many requirements, including that a Pueblo Mágico be within a two-hour drive of another consolidated tourist destination, with “reasonably good roads”. This is in the best interests of both the Ministry of Tourism and the tourists of the area. It precludes many villages blooming with culture and character in the mountains of Oaxaca and Chiapas. (Clausen and Gyimóthy 321-322).

Next

Requirements

The Ministry of Tourism is strict on its requirements. To be considered for the program, a town must be able to pass on several points. Preservation of local heritage is important and therefore they must avoid innovation to an extent. Local residents are required to be involved in the projects. Habits and customs, as well as symbolic attributes and original architecture must be preserved. Outside of the extent of these requirements, the town must have a minimum population of 20,000 people and be located less than 200 kilometers from the nearest city (Uhnák 9).

Benefits

Alongside the preservation of local culture, and incentivization of tourism, government funding also greatly helps to improve the locals’ lives. Funding from the program provides jobs in the form of infrastructure projects which also provide education, clean water and sanitation. Increased tourism results in an inflow of money to the local economy and increases revenue for common tourist industries such as hotels, restaurants, and guided tours. The artisanal market is supported by sales of local crafts and entrepreneurship is encouraged.(Zocalo).

Next

Funding

The Ministry of Tourism provides a tremendous amount of resources to be made available to designated Pueblos Mágicos. In 2010, the Ministry provided 1,900,000 Mexican Pesos to 35 towns in the program- nearly US$76,000 on average to each town. This funding allows these towns to transform cultural centers such as museums, and churches to be rehabilitated. The funding helps to maintain local culture from slow degradation, as well as make the towns more attractive for tourists (Zocalo).

Next

Government Benefits

Aside from the advantages of cultural awareness and a vast increase in business for the local economies, Pueblos Mágicos also benefit from the government. Coronado states that once a town is given the designation of Pueblo Mágico, its’ name is made available everywhere in the form of promotions, and guides for Mexico’s visitors. This provides visibility to incoming tourists. Travel agencies can help promote a more small-town tourist experience rather than direct focus into large cities. This reduces the load on overcrowded tourist cities and prevents them from becoming over barren and uncultured due to the large annual inflow of visitors (Coronado).

Next

Requirements for Funding

Although the government does provide support for these Pueblos Mágicos, they have several requirements in place to prevent municipalities from seeking funding without effort on their part. This program provides funding for infrastructure and training projects. Projects are only approved when they are deemed relevant by the Ministry of Tourism and they are not guaranteed. Every project that is approved must have, as an end goal, the tourists experience in mind (Coronado).

Next

Their Impact

Since the program’s initiation in 2001, over 80 towns have been accepted and enrolled. The majority of these towns were elected due to their indigenous origins or colonial legacy. Some towns were enrolled in order to preserve their traditional organizational principles. A select few have significant historical events that bring meaning to the visitors (Clausen and Gyimóthy 322).

Culture and Profit

The programs do well to support the local population, however there may be downsides. There remains speculation that competition for tourist money increases the likelihood of trying to sell something vivid over replicating actual culture and heritage. Religious practices, through external influence, can even lose their own meaning. Music, dance, clothing, and language can be amplified to support the present scenery for the tourist’s sake, rather than being just a natural part of local culture (Ramírez and Espinosa 222).
